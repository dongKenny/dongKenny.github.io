Japanese Dialects

This is a project which aims to (non-exhaustively) showcase the differences between "Standard Japanese" (標準語 - hyōjungo) and dialects (弁 - ben), illustrate grammatical changes, and highlight interesting colloquialisms

Hyōjungo

The "textbook" Japanese which became standardized after the Meiji Restoration. It is the standard Japanese taught in schools, but people in every region of Japan will speak in their respective regional dialect.

Meiji Restoration in 1868, the government attempted to create a strong centralized state. Linguistic unification was a step toward shaping the national identity. Through the national educational system and the military, a dominant national dialect replaced local and regional dialects. The resulting dialect, hyōjungo (‘the standard language’), was based on the linguistic patterns of Tōkyō’s samurai (‘warrior’) classes and has become the norm in the educational system, the mass media, government, and business.
Theodore Bestor, Japan in Countries and their Cultures Vol. 2

だ --> や 静かだったら 静かやったら

ない --> ん 知らない 知らん
(い-adj) い --> か 面白い 面白か
(い-adj) い --> か いい よか
ている --> (ます form)よる 食べている 食べよる
よ --> ばい それだよ それやばい
ないで/ないの --> んと 行かないで 行かんと
ないで/ないの --> んと しないの せんと
